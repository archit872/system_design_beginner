System Design — A Beginner’s Book

Build the vocabulary, mental models, and workflows to discuss and sketch real systems. You’ll learn fundamentals—clients/servers, storage, caching, scalability, and reliability—then consolidate with a guided capstone.

How to Use This Book

This book is for software engineers with basic programming skills who want a practical entry point into system design. Each chapter follows a consistent rhythm: short explanations, concrete examples, trade-offs, a mini case study, and a clear practice block with success criteria. The goal isn’t to memorize buzzwords—it’s to develop a working mental model that transfers from interviews to day-to-day engineering.

Read linearly if you’re brand new. If you’ve seen some of the material, skim the “Objectives” at the top of each chapter and jump straight to the Practice and Mastery blocks. Expect to pause frequently to diagram, estimate, or rewrite definitions in your own words. When a term appears in glossary style, hover to preview or click through to the full entry.

For motivation, we include a capstone in Chapter 7 you can complete in a few sittings. You’ll select a problem (e.g., URL shortener), gather requirements, draft a high-level design, make storage/caching/scaling choices, and present decisions with trade-offs. This mirrors real design discussions and gives you a portfolio artifact.

Study cadence. Aim for 3–5 short sessions per week. Each chapter’s practice takes ~30–60 minutes. Repetition beats cramming; return to your capstone design after each chapter to refine it.

What You’ll Learn

  • Core mental models: clients, servers, protocols, and data flow.
  • Storage choices: relational vs. NoSQL, indexing, replication, and consistency trade-offs.
  • Performance: caching strategies (browser, CDN, server), invalidation patterns, and latency budgets.
  • Scalability: vertical vs. horizontal scaling, load balancing, stateless services, and bottleneck hunting.
  • Reliability: redundancy, failover, backups, and the basics of monitoring and alerting.

How to Get the Most Out of It

  1. Define terms in your own words. Each chapter’s Mastery block includes self-check prompts with sample answers.
  2. Draw diagrams. Even rough boxes-and-arrows clarify assumptions and surface constraints.
  3. Practice trade-offs. For every design choice, list at least one alternative and why you didn’t pick it.
  4. Iterate the capstone. After each chapter, add or refine a piece of your capstone design.

What to Prepare

Tools

  • A simple diagramming tool (paper + pen is fine).
  • A language/runtime you’re comfortable with for tiny servers (Node, Python, Go, Java, etc.).
  • Terminal and browser devtools for quick experiments and timing.

Mindset

System design is constraint-driven storytelling. You’ll weigh requirements (functional and non-functional) against trade-offs (performance, cost, complexity) to reach a coherent design. You don’t need perfection—you need a defensible narrative that aligns with goals.

Progression Map

Beginner → Intermediate

Beginner End Goal: comfortably discuss and sketch small systems (e.g., URL shortener), use terms like latency, throughput, scalability, and explain basic trade-offs.

Intermediate Entry: extend designs to medium-scale systems (feeds, chat, messaging). Introduce patterns like sharding, leader-follower replication, idempotency, and backpressure.

Advanced Path: global scale, distributed consensus, correctness under failures, and performance engineering informed by measurement.

Quick Start Checklist 10–15 min
  1. Skim all chapter objectives.
  2. Pick a capstone (URL shortener recommended).
  3. Write 5–7 requirements and 3 success metrics.
  4. Draw a first-pass high-level diagram.
  5. After each chapter, update the design with new ideas.
