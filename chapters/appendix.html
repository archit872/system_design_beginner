APPENDIX

Progression Map & Checklist

Use this appendix as your after-care kit: a progression roadmap, a “top 10 pitfalls” cheat sheet with quick fixes, and review checklists. Bookmark it and revisit before interviews or design docs.

Progression Map

Plan: For the next 6–8 weeks, pick two medium-scale systems (messaging and news feed). Design each twice: baseline, then 10× scale.

Top 10 Pitfalls (and Quick Fixes)

  1. Skipping requirements. Fix: Write 3–5 functional + 3 non-functional goals with numbers (p95, RPS, storage).
  2. Over-engineering early. Fix: Start with a monolith + DB; add cache/LB only after measuring.
  3. Confusing latency vs. throughput. Fix: Track both; optimize p95 and p99 for UX.
  4. Ignoring failures. Fix: Add one redundancy per critical component + a graceful degradation path.
  5. Premature sharding. Fix: Exhaust indexes and caching first; design shard key with access patterns.
  6. Sticky sessions everywhere. Fix: Externalize session state (JWT/Redis) to keep services stateless.
  7. Cache invalidation laziness. Fix: Choose TTL vs. explicit purge; document keys and who owns invalidation.
  8. Replica-induced staleness. Fix: “Read your writes” policy or bounded-staleness reads for writers.
  9. Sync analytics on the hot path. Fix: Emit to a queue, batch process, display eventual counts.
  10. No observability. Fix: Define SLIs/SLOs, instrument tracing, and set alerts on user-facing symptoms.

Review Checklist (Before You Call a Design “Done”)

  • Requirements enumerated with numbers (p95, availability, data retention).
  • Back-of-the-envelope estimates (RPS, storage, cache hit goal) recorded.
  • Baseline architecture drawn (edge/CDN → LB → app → cache → DB).
  • Data model + indexes match top queries.
  • Failure modes listed; redundancy/failover noted.
  • Caching & invalidation strategy chosen and documented.
  • Operational plan: monitoring metrics, alerts, dashboards, runbooks.
  • Security & abuse considerations (rate limits, validation, privacy).
  • Clear trade-offs and non-goals explicitly stated.

From Beginner → Intermediate: A 4-Week Study Path

  1. Week 1: Re-do TinyURL with 10× traffic; add abuse controls; set SLOs and alerts.
  2. Week 2: Design a messaging app. Cover ordering, delivery, eventual consistency, and read replicas.
  3. Week 3: Design a news feed. Explore fanout, caching timelines, and ranking latency budgets.
  4. Week 4: Add a passive region + DNS failover to one design; practice incident response (runbook).
Output rule: Each week produces a 1–2 page design doc, one diagram, and a brief “trade-offs” section.

Reusable Templates

Design Doc Skeleton

1. Problem & Requirements
2. Non-Functional Goals & SLOs
3. Estimates (RPS, storage, cache hit)
4. Architecture (components & diagram)
5. Data Model & Indexes
6. Read/Write Paths
7. Reliability (redundancy, failover, backups)
8. Caching & Invalidation
9. Security & Abuse
10. Observability (metrics, alerts, runbooks)
11. Trade-offs & Alternatives
12. Risks & Open Questions

Incident Runbook Skeleton

1. Symptoms (SLIs, dashboards)
2. Probable Causes (by dependency)
3. Immediate Actions (rate limit, circuit breakers)
4. Verification Steps
5. Rollback/Workarounds
6. Communication (internal, status page)
7. Postmortem Checklist

Suggested Reading (Next Level)

  • Designing Data-Intensive Applications (Kleppmann) — deep dives into storage, streams, and consistency.
  • Google SRE Book — reliability culture and practices.
  • System Design Primer — case studies to rehearse patterns and trade-offs.

Final Note

System design is a craft. Aim for clarity, measure impacts, and narrate trade-offs. Repetition builds fluency; incidents build intuition; reading builds breadth. Keep your notebook growing—designs improve faster than you think.
