Chapter 1 Orientation

Orientation — What is System Design?

This chapter defines system design, shows how it appears in interviews versus real engineering, and introduces the goals that anchor every design decision: scalability, reliability, and maintainability. You’ll also learn the core vocabulary that we’ll use throughout the book.

Prerequisites & Learning Objectives

Prerequisites

  • Basic programming knowledge in any language.
  • Comfort with the command line and running a local web server.
  • Curiosity about how large apps work under constraints (time, cost, scale).

Objectives

  • Explain what system design is and why it matters in industry.
  • Differentiate interview-style design from real-world practice.
  • Use common terms—latency, throughput, availability—accurately.
  • Identify how scalability, reliability, and maintainability steer design choices.

1.1 What System Design Means in Industry

At its core, system design is the process of turning requirements into a cohesive architecture under constraints. The constraints include traffic levels, data growth, latency budgets, regulatory obligations, cost of goods sold (COGS), team skills, and deadlines. Unlike pure algorithmic problems, the “answer” is rarely unique. Good design is a defensible narrative supported by measurements and trade-offs.

Industry work usually starts with a problem statement (“We need a notifications service for 50k DAU”) and ends with an implementation plan. Along the way, you’ll analyze non-functional requirements, choose technologies, sketch data flows, estimate capacity, and propose operational guardrails (rate limits, retries, circuit breakers). It is as much about communication as technology: writing clear design docs and aligning stakeholders is a core competency.

Analogy: System design is like city planning. Roads (APIs), traffic rules (protocols), buildings (services), utilities (databases, caches), and emergency services (monitoring, on-call) must coordinate. You can expand a city district-by-district if you keep future growth and resilience in mind.

Example: Suppose your company expects a 10× traffic spike during a festival sale. You might pre-warm caches, add auto-scaling rules, and shed non-critical workloads. You’d also cap per-user request rates and prioritize cart/checkout APIs over recommendation refreshes. The design balances reliability under load and maintainability by avoiding ad-hoc hacks.

Counter-example: A team chooses a single-node database for a chat product to “avoid complexity.” It works for a pilot but fails under growth; adding sharding later is expensive. Here the design optimized early for simplicity but ignored scalability and availability needs.

1.2 System Design in Interviews vs. Practice

Interviews compress months of engineering into 30–60 minutes. You won’t build anything; you’ll communicate a clear approach. The signals interviewers look for include: structured requirements gathering, identification of bottlenecks, sane data modeling, awareness of caching and load leveling, and explicit trade-offs. You’ll rarely dive deep into vendor-specific details.

In practice, you’ll prototype, run load tests, and iterate with metrics. Reliability work (e.g., SLOs, error budgets) and operational plans (alerts, dashboards, runbooks) carry heavy weight. Interview designs can be elegant but brittle; production designs must handle failures, migrations, and human error.

Pitfall

Over-indexing on novelty. In interviews, proposing Kafka + Cassandra + gRPC + Kubernetes for a tiny feature can sound impressive, but it may ignore the simplest solution that meets requirements. In practice, unnecessary complexity harms maintainability and increases failure modes.

1.3 Core Goals: Scalability, Reliability, Maintainability

These goals provide a compass for decisions. When stuck, ask: Which goal am I optimizing right now, and what am I trading off?

Scalability

Plain: Will it keep up when usage grows? Formal: The property that a system can handle increased load by adding resources, often horizontally. Pitfall: Confusing burst handling with sustained scaling. Example: A stateless API behind a load balancer lets you add replicas to increase throughput.

Reliability

Plain: Does it keep working when things break? Formal: The probability a system performs its intended function over a period, often measured via SLA/SLO. Pitfall: Treating retries as a fix-all; without idempotency and backoff, retries amplify incidents. Example: Replication and automated failover keep a service available during a node failure.

Maintainability

Plain: How easily can we change and operate it? Formal: The degree of effort required to modify, test, deploy, and troubleshoot. Pitfall: Accumulating hidden operational complexity (hand-tuned shards, bespoke queues). Example: Well-factored services with clear APIs and dashboards reduce cognitive load for new contributors.

⚖️ Trade-off: Adding heavy consistency guarantees (e.g., serializable transactions across regions) may improve correctness but increase latency and cost, harming scalability. Choose the weakest consistency that satisfies user needs.

1.4 Key Vocabulary

API
Plain: How one piece of software asks another to do something. Formal: A contract of request/response schemas and semantics (REST/RPC/GraphQL).
Latency
Plain: How long you wait for a response. Formal: End-to-end time between request and response, often summarized by percentiles (p50, p95).
Throughput
Plain: How much work gets done per second. Formal: Rate of completed requests or processed items per time unit.
Availability
Plain: Chance the system is up. Formal: Proportion of time a service meets its SLO (e.g., 99.9%).
Durability
Plain: Data sticks around. Formal: Probability of data survival over time, often via replication and backups.
Consistency
Plain: Users see the “same truth.” Formal: Guarantees about reads/writes order and visibility (eventual, strong, causal).
Partition
Plain: The network splits. Formal: Loss of communication between nodes; prompts CAP trade-offs.
Idempotency
Plain: Doing the same request twice has the same effect. Formal: Operation semantics where repeated execution yields an equivalent state.
Backpressure
Plain: Slow down the sender to avoid overload. Formal: Mechanisms that regulate producers when consumers lag.
Observability
Plain: Can we understand what’s happening? Formal: The ability to infer internal state via logs, metrics, and traces.

You’ll see these terms throughout the book. Full entries appear in the Glossary.

Case Study: “Morning Commute” of a Request (≈200 words)

Imagine opening a mobile app to view your notifications. Your client issues an HTTPS request to an API endpoint. A load balancer selects a healthy replica of the notifications service. Because the service is stateless, any replica can serve the request; user state lives in a database and cache. The service queries a cache for the user’s unread count (fast path). On a miss, it hits the primary database with an indexed query, then writes the result into the cache with a short TTL.

To personalize, the service fans out to a “preferences” microservice via RPC. If that dependency is slow, a client-side timeout triggers fallbacks (e.g., default settings) to keep the overall latency within the budget. Metrics capture p95 latency and error rates; distributed tracing ties the hop sequence together. If a dependency fails outright, a circuit breaker opens and requests use cached results. The response is returned to the client, and a CDN caches static assets to reduce repeat downloads. In ~200 ms, your inbox appears—an orchestration of scalability (replicas, CDN), reliability (circuit breaker, retries), and maintainability (statelessness, observability).

Mastery Check Self-assessment

Short Quiz (with sample answers)

1) Define latency in your own words and give a scenario where p95 matters more than average.

Sample answer: Latency is the time between sending a request and receiving the response. p95 matters for user experience because a few very slow requests dominate perceived performance—e.g., checkout flows where 1 in 20 users see a spinner for 3 seconds.

2) Define throughput and explain its tension with latency.

Sample answer: Throughput is work completed per unit time. Batching improves throughput but increases per-request latency (e.g., processing 100 messages at once). Conversely, prioritizing low latency may reduce batching opportunities and total throughput.

3) What does scalability mean? Provide a concrete example.

Sample answer: The ability to handle increased load by adding resources. Example: A stateless API can scale horizontally by adding pods behind a load balancer.

4) How do interviews differ from production design in terms of evidence?

Sample answer: Interviews rely on reasoned estimates, while production design requires evidence from benchmarks, staging canaries, load tests, and production metrics.

5) Give a plain + formal definition of idempotency and one pitfall.

Sample answer: Plain: repeating a request doesn’t double-charge or duplicate effects. Formal: running the same operation multiple times yields an equivalent state. Pitfall: forgetting to include a request identifier so retries create duplicates.
