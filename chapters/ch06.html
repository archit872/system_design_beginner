CHAPTER 6

Reliability & Fault Tolerance

Reliability means your system works when users need it. Fault tolerance is the art of surviving failures gracefully. In this chapter you’ll learn redundancy techniques, failover and disaster recovery, and the basics of monitoring and alerting. You’ll adapt your scalable web app to add resilience against crashes and outages.

Learning Objectives

  • Identify common failure modes in distributed systems.
  • Explain replication, redundancy, and backups.
  • Describe failover and disaster recovery basics.
  • Apply monitoring and alerting for critical SLIs.

6.1 Why Systems Fail

Systems fail due to hardware crashes, software bugs, misconfigurations, overload, or external factors (network partitions, DDoS). Reliability engineering accepts that failures are inevitable and designs to mitigate blast radius and recovery time.

Analogy: Airplanes have multiple engines and backup instruments. Even if one fails, the plane continues safely.
Pitfall: Assuming cloud providers guarantee 100% uptime. They offer high availability zones, but you must architect across them.

6.2 Replication, Redundancy & Backups

Replication ensures multiple copies of data. Redundancy duplicates components (servers, networks) so one can fail without outage. Backups protect against corruption or deletion.

Compare & Contrast

TechniquePurposeTrade-off
ReplicationHigh availability, read scalingLag, consistency issues
RedundancyResilience to component failureHigher cost
BackupsDisaster recovery, corruption fixRestore may be slow
Case study: A fintech keeps hot replicas in the same region for fast failover, plus cold backups in another region for disaster recovery. This layered approach balances RTO (minutes) and cost.

6.3 Fault-Tolerant Design Patterns

  • Failover: Automatic switch to replica/standby on failure.
  • Graceful degradation: Offer limited service when dependencies fail (e.g., show cached timeline without live updates).
  • Retries with backoff: Retry transient failures, but avoid thundering herd.
  • Circuit breaker: Cut traffic to failing dependency, return fallback.
  • Bulkheads: Isolate components to limit cascading failures.
Trade-off: Too aggressive retries can overload a recovering service. Design with exponential backoff and jitter.

6.4 Monitoring, Logging & Alerts Basics

Monitoring collects metrics (CPU, latency, error rate). Logging records detailed events. Alerting notifies humans when SLOs are violated.

Key Metrics (Golden Signals)

  • Latency: Time to serve a request.
  • Traffic: Requests per second.
  • Errors: Error rate (%).
  • Saturation: Resource usage (CPU, memory, threads).
Mini Case Study: A video platform sets an SLO: 99.9% of streams must start under 5 seconds. Monitoring reveals p95=4s but p99=15s in one region. Investigation shows packet loss. They add regional redundancy and CDN tuning. Result: p99 drops under 6s. Lesson: reliability means serving the long tail, not just the median.

Practice

  1. Failure mapping (25–30 min): List 5 possible failures in your messaging app (server crash, DB outage, cache miss storm). For each, propose mitigation. Success: Each failure has at least one redundancy/failover pattern.
  2. Monitoring design (20–25 min): Choose 3 SLIs (latency, error rate, traffic). Sketch a dashboard. Success: Includes thresholds and alert policy.
  3. Chaos drill (30 min): Simulate a DB outage (hypothetically or via chaos tool). Write how your app should behave. Success: Degraded but not total outage; recovery path clear.

Mastery Check

  1. Define: What is fault tolerance?
    Sample answer

    The ability of a system to continue functioning correctly even when some components fail.

  2. Explain: Difference between replication and backups?
    Sample answer

    Replication makes live copies for availability; backups are offline copies for disaster recovery and corruption recovery.

  3. Apply: YouTube must survive a server crash. What redundancy would you add?
    Sample answer

    Multiple app servers behind a load balancer, DB replicas, CDN for video. If one server dies, traffic shifts seamlessly.

  4. Evaluate: Why are alerts on CPU spikes alone insufficient?
    Sample answer

    High CPU may not impact users; what matters are SLIs like latency and error rate. Alerts should align to SLOs.

  5. Explain: What is graceful degradation?
    Sample answer

    Maintaining partial functionality during failure (e.g., cached timeline without live chat).

  6. Apply: A cache cluster fails. How do you avoid total outage?
    Sample answer

    Fallback to DB reads with rate limiting; circuit breaker to shed load if DB saturates.
