CHAPTER 8

Pitfalls, Review & Next Steps

System design mastery is less about memorizing diagrams and more about avoiding **common traps**. This chapter summarizes frequent beginner pitfalls, reviews vocabulary and patterns, and helps you plan the journey from **beginner → intermediate**.

8.1 Common Pitfalls

  • Over-engineering: Adding microservices, queues, and caches before validating requirements. Fix: Start with simple monolith + DB.
  • Ignoring requirements: Jumping into diagrams without clarifying functional vs. non-functional needs. Fix: Write down top-3 constraints first.
  • Neglecting trade-offs: Presenting “perfect” designs. Fix: Always mention what you did NOT choose and why.
  • Forgetting failures: Assuming dependencies are always up. Fix: Add at least one redundancy/failover per critical component.
  • Vague vocabulary: Using “fast” or “scalable” without numbers. Fix: Attach metrics: “p95 latency < 100 ms”.
Trade-off: Simplicity vs. future-proofing. Too simple → rewrite soon; too complex → slow, costly. The skill is choosing the right level for the problem.

8.2 Review of Key Vocabulary & Patterns

Let’s recap the 8–10 most important terms you should be able to explain to a peer:

  1. Latency: Delay per request; users feel it directly.
  2. Throughput: Requests/second; capacity measure.
  3. Cache: Store hot data closer; trade-off = invalidation.
  4. SQL vs. NoSQL: Strong schema vs. flexible, consistent vs. eventual consistency.
  5. Load balancer: Spreads load, improves availability.
  6. Stateless vs. Stateful: Impacts scaling ease.
  7. Replication & redundancy: Multiple copies for resilience.
  8. SLO & SLI: Targets and measurements for reliability.
Mini Case Study: A junior engineer interviewing for a backend role nailed vocabulary—latency, throughput, SLOs—but couldn’t show trade-offs. The interviewer advised: “Words show you studied; trade-offs show you practiced.” She re-did designs, always naming 1–2 things she’d cut. Within months, her answers were stronger and she landed offers.

8.3 Transition Path: Beginner → Intermediate

Intermediate designers tackle **medium-scale systems**: chat apps, feeds, payment services. They master **patterns** like sharding, eventual consistency, and queues.

  • Build 2–3 more designs (messaging, news feed, e-commerce cart).
  • Read Designing Data-Intensive Applications (Kleppmann).
  • Practice scaling one system 10×: re-estimate, redraw, add new trade-offs.
  • Introduce cost-awareness: compare solutions by $$$ at scale.
Analogy: Beginner = driving in a parking lot. Intermediate = navigating a city with traffic. You know the controls; now you must plan routes under constraints.

8.4 Suggested Project Extensions

Extend your capstone (TinyURL or chat app) with one of these:

  • Analytics: Add daily click dashboards (batch processing).
  • Abuse detection: Add rate limiting and basic heuristics.
  • Multi-region: Add a passive region with DNS failover.
  • Mobile API: Add token-based auth and pagination.

Each forces new trade-offs: storage schema changes, cache invalidation, replication lag, and auth flows.

Practice

  1. Design review (30–40 min): Pick your TinyURL design. Identify 2–3 mistakes (e.g., TTL mis-match, missing abuse checks). Success: Each mistake mapped to a fix.
  2. Glossary drill (20–25 min): Write definitions + micro-examples for 10 terms. Success: Each plain + formal + example.
  3. Intermediate sketch (40–60 min): Draft design for a messaging app. Success: Must include sharding, eventual consistency, and scaling strategy.

Mastery Check

  1. Define: What is over-engineering?
    Sample answer

    Adding unnecessary complexity (microservices, queues) before requirements justify them.

  2. Explain: Why write requirements first?
    Sample answer

    They anchor trade-offs. Without them you design in a vacuum and risk missing critical goals.

  3. Apply: You must scale TinyURL 10×. What’s your first step?
    Sample answer

    Re-estimate traffic/storage; check if CDN caching suffices; only then add DB sharding or async pipelines.

  4. Evaluate: Vocabulary vs. trade-offs — which signals maturity?
    Sample answer

    Vocabulary shows knowledge; trade-offs show reasoning. The latter is more important in interviews and real design.

  5. Apply: You designed with no failover. DB dies. What happens?
    Sample answer

    Outage until manual fix. With replication/failover you’d redirect traffic automatically, minimizing downtime.

Recap & Next Steps

You’ve completed the beginner curriculum. You can discuss small systems, identify scalability and reliability levers, and avoid classic pitfalls. Your next step: design medium-scale systems, incorporate advanced patterns, and study real-world case studies. Keep practicing trade-offs — that’s the heart of system design.
